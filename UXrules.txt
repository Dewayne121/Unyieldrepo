1. **Visibility of system status**
   Keep users informed about what’s happening with clear, timely feedback so they can understand results and decide next steps.
   Do: show status and progress; respond quickly; don’t take consequential actions silently.

2. **Match between the system and the real world**
   Use the user’s language and real-world conventions; organize information naturally.
   Do: avoid internal jargon; use familiar terms/icons; validate wording and mental models with user research.

3. **User control and freedom**
   Make it easy to undo, redo, cancel, and exit unwanted states.
   Do: provide clear, labeled escape routes; support Undo/Redo; don’t trap users in flows.

4. **Consistency and standards**
   Use consistent patterns and follow platform/industry conventions so users don’t have to relearn.
   Do: keep internal consistency across screens; align with common UI standards.

5. **Error prevention**
   Prevent errors before they happen using constraints, good defaults, validation, and confirmations for risky actions.
   Do: stop high-cost errors first; reduce slips with guardrails; reduce mistakes by aligning with users’ expectations.

6. **Recognition rather than recall**
   Minimize memory load by keeping options, labels, and needed info visible or easily retrievable.
   Do: show choices and context; use cues and defaults; avoid forcing users to remember across steps.

7. **Flexibility and efficiency of use**
   Support both novices and experts with accelerators and customization.
   Do: add shortcuts (keyboard/gestures); allow personalization; enable configurable workflows for frequent tasks.

8. **Aesthetic and minimalist design**
   Remove anything unnecessary; every extra element competes with what matters.
   Do: prioritize primary tasks and content; reduce clutter; keep visuals supporting the goal.

9. **Help users recover from errors**
   Write error messages in plain language that say what happened, why, and how to fix it; make them noticeable.
   Do: avoid codes/jargon; highlight clearly; provide actionable next steps.

10. **Help and documentation**
    When help is needed, make it searchable, task-focused, concise, and step-by-step—ideally in context.
    Do: surface help at the moment of need; keep instructions concrete and short.
## Aesthetic-Usability Effect (usable takeaways)

**Core idea**
Users tend to perceive visually attractive interfaces as more usable and are more tolerant of *minor* usability issues when the UI looks good. This can **hide real problems** in usability testing, so you have to judge issues by **observed behavior**, not just what users say.

### What it does to UX

* Attractive visuals create a positive emotional response.
* That positive feeling increases patience and forgiveness for small friction.
* A “good UX” isn’t only functional—visual design strongly shapes perceived quality and usability.

### Limits

* Aesthetic appeal does **not** compensate for **major** usability problems.
* If functionality is sacrificed for looks, users lose patience once tasks get hard or repeated friction stacks up.
* Form and function must work together; aesthetics should support content and task completion.

### Usability-testing problem it creates

* Participants may struggle, fail, or get stuck—then still rate the experience highly or talk only about colors/photography.
* Result: usability issues are harder to surface if you rely on post-task ratings or general “looks great” feedback.

### How to spot it in research

Compare:

* **What users do** (errors, hesitation, backtracking, time on task, task failure, confusion)
  vs.
* **What users say** (“looks nice,” “love the colors”) especially when it doesn’t match observed difficulty.

When you get positive visual comments after observed struggle, consider three causes:

1. They feel pressure to say *something* (silence discomfort).
2. They feel pressure to be *nice* (think you built it).
3. The **aesthetic-usability effect** is masking usability problems.

### How to reduce false-positive “it’s great” feedback

**Reduce pressure to comment**

* Set a low-stress tone early; reassure them their actions are helpful even if quiet.
* Allow silence; don’t force constant talk.
* Ask open-ended questions, but don’t push if they have nothing.

**Reduce pressure to compliment**

* Create distance from the design: say you’re here to learn (don’t lie about your role).
* Stay neutral in facial expressions/body language to avoid reinforcing praise.

**Work around the effect (probe beyond visuals without leading)**
Use broad prompts like:

* “Any comments about how easy or difficult it was to find this information?”
* “What made this easy or difficult to read?”
* “What would you change about this app, if anything?”
  Optionally revisit a difficult screen and ask them to describe what happened.
* Don’t push too hard—over-probing can make people invent answers.

### Practical conclusion

* Invest in visual design: it increases approachability, patience, and perceived professionalism.
* Don’t let attractive UI “pass” in research by default—treat behavior as the truth and investigate mismatches between praise and performance.
## Source of truth for “good-looking” UX that is also usable

### 1) Start with usability fundamentals, then express them visually

Aesthetic quality should reinforce usability, not compete with it. The most durable baseline is Nielsen Norman Group’s usability heuristics (especially **Consistency and standards**, and **Aesthetic and minimalist design**). ([Nielsen Norman Group][1])

**Rule:** Every visual element must justify itself by supporting a user task; remove “noise” that dilutes what matters. ([Nielsen Norman Group][2])

---

### 2) Build a clear visual hierarchy

Good aesthetics are largely **hierarchy**: users should instantly see what’s primary, secondary, and optional.

Common patterns across major guidelines:

* Emphasize content first; controls should not overpower content.
* Make the primary action visually dominant and unambiguous.
* Use progressive disclosure: show essentials now, details when needed.
  Apple explicitly calls out establishing clear visual hierarchy. ([Apple Developer][3])

---

### 3) Use a “foundations” system (typography, spacing, color) instead of ad-hoc styling

The strongest design systems converge on the same approach: define foundations once, then reuse everywhere via tokens/styles.

#### Typography

* Create a small set of text styles with consistent size/weight/line-height and clear roles (e.g., display, heading, body, label).
* Optimize for readability (line height, hierarchy, predictable rhythm). Material and GOV.UK both emphasize readable type systems and consistent application. ([Material Design][4])
* Maintain a consistent vertical rhythm (Fluent’s typography guidance emphasizes baseline alignment and rhythm). ([Fluent 2 Design System][5])

#### Spacing and layout

* Use a spacing scale (often 8px-based) and avoid “mystery values.”
* Tokens for spacing keep layouts consistent and easier to evolve. Atlassian explicitly uses an 8px base unit; Carbon uses tokenized spacing scales. ([carbondesignsystem.com][6])
* Layout guidance in platform HIGs tends to favor restrained density and avoiding crowded clusters of controls. ([Apple Developer][7])

#### Color

* Color should communicate **hierarchy, state, and meaning** (not decoration).
* Prefer semantic roles (e.g., success/warning/error) and consistent mapping.
* Material’s color system emphasizes accessible schemes that communicate hierarchy and state. ([Material Design][8])

---

### 4) Treat accessibility as a design constraint, not a later compliance pass

Accessible visuals tend to look “cleaner” and behave better in real conditions (glare, small screens, fatigue).

Minimum expectations:

* **Text contrast:** WCAG requires at least **4.5:1** for normal text and **3:1** for large text (AA). ([W3C][9])
* **Keyboard focus:** Focus indicators must be clearly visible, and focused elements should not be obscured. ([W3C][10])
* Validate quickly with a contrast checker during design and review. ([webaim.org][11])

---

### 5) Use platform guidelines to match user expectations

For apps, “good aesthetics” are partly “looks native enough to feel obvious.”

* **Apple HIG**: platform conventions, hierarchy, layout, and UI patterns. ([Apple Developer][3])
* **Material Design 3**: typography + color systems and component styling. ([Material Design][12])
* **Microsoft Fluent 2**: typography rhythm and token-driven consistency. ([Fluent 2 Design System][5])

---

### 6) Systematize with design tokens (single source of truth across design + code)

To prevent visual drift:

* Store core decisions (color, type, spacing, radii, elevation) as **tokens**.
* Connect design tools and implementation through token standards.
  Figma describes tokens as a “single source of truth,” and Fluent/Carbon/Atlassian all reinforce tokenized foundations. ([help.figma.com][13])
  For interoperability, the Design Tokens Community Group is standardizing token exchange formats. ([designtokens.org][14])

---

### 7) Validate aesthetics with research, because aesthetics can mislead

Users often *perceive* attractive interfaces as more usable (the aesthetic-usability effect), which can mask real usability problems during testing. ([Nielsen Norman Group][15])
**Implication:** In usability tests, rely on observed behavior and task outcomes, not only user sentiment.

---

## A practical “good UX aesthetics” review checklist

Use this as a quick audit:

**Hierarchy**

* Primary action is obvious within 1–2 seconds.
* Screens have one main purpose; secondary content is visually quieter. ([Apple Developer][3])

**Typography**

* Limited set of text styles; consistent usage; readable line heights. ([Material Design][4])

**Spacing**

* Consistent spacing scale; alignment is intentional; no one-off padding/margins. ([carbondesignsystem.com][6])

**Color**

* Color conveys meaning/state; contrast passes WCAG; disabled vs enabled is clear. ([Material Design][8])

**Accessibility & interaction**

* Focus indicators are visible; focus isn’t hidden by sticky UI; key flows work with keyboard. ([W3C][10])

**Consistency**

* Components and patterns are reused; tokens/styles drive implementation. ([help.figma.com][13])
